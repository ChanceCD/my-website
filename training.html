Welcome

This single‑file training walks you through the essentials of the Canada.ca Content Style Guide: what it is, who must use it, and how to apply it in your day‑to‑day writing for the web.

Why it matters: The Style Guide helps make government content easy to find, easy to understand, and easy to use for everyone. You’ll practice with quick examples and finish with a checklist you can print.

Core principles

Help people complete tasks

Most visitors come to complete a task. Prioritize what helps them act: eligibility, steps, deadlines, contact, and what to do next.

Consider audience needs

Write for accessibility, inclusivity, and readability. Avoid barriers like long blocks of text, jargon, and unnecessarily complex sentences.

Tips to apply it
  • Lead with the most important action or message on each page.
  • Design for phones first—keep paragraphs and headings short.
  • Provide equivalent content in English and French.

Plain language

Plain language is required. It means people can quickly find what they need, understand it, and use it. Use the inverted pyramid and keep sentences short.

Needs work

Stakeholders are hereby advised that pursuant to the aforementioned regulations, applicants are obligated to furnish documentary evidence prior to initialization of the process.

Plain language

Before you start: You must show your documents to apply. Bring a photo ID and proof of address.

Checklist

  • Start with what matters most (inverted pyramid).
  • Use familiar words and short sentences.
  • Prefer active, positive phrasing.
  • Cut jargon and noun strings (replace with clear verbs).

Tone

Speak to the person

Use second person (you, your) in titles, navigation, and body text.

Change your address • Replace your card

Use “we” for Government of Canada

Write from the GC perspective. Name a specific institution only when people must contact it or it plays a key role.

Expressing requirements

  • Legal requirement: use must — “You must submit your claim within 30 days.”
  • Administrative requirement: use need to — “You need to bring photo ID.”

Style conventions

Capitalization & punctuation

  • Use sentence case for headings and navigation.
  • Capitalize formal names (acts, regulations, program and institution names).
  • Keep punctuation simple (avoid unnecessary commas and colons).

Words in transition

  • website (not web site)
  • web page (not webpage)
  • email (not e‑mail)
  • online (not on line)
  • web (lowercase)

Structuring content

Page titles & headings

  • Describe what’s on the page (avoid slogans or clever phrasing).
  • Use one clear H1 per page; use H2/H3 to show structure.
  • Include keywords people actually search for.

Lists & tables

  • Prefer short vertical lists (aim for ≤7 items).
  • Be consistent in list grammar; keep one idea per bullet.
  • Use tables only for data, with proper row/column headers.
SEO‑adjacent: metadata

Use your H1 as the title tag. Write a short, descriptive meta description with natural keywords.

Links

Links should be descriptive and able to stand alone. Use unique link text when links go to different destinations, and indicate downloads.

Needs work
  • Click here
  • More information
  • Form (PDF)
Descriptive
  • Check if you’re eligible
  • Apply for the caregiver benefit
  • Download the caregiver benefit form (PDF, 1.2 MB)

Note: If a public page must link to content available only on the GC network, add “(accessible only on the Government of Canada network)” after the link.

Images & video

  • Provide meaningful alt text for informative images.
  • Use captions or transcripts for multimedia.
  • Ensure images and multimedia are equivalent in English and French (or provide equivalent information in both languages).

Bilingual requirements

Under the Official Languages Act, publish communications and services to the public in both official languages. Translation should reflect Canadian conventions in English and French. Provide equivalent information in both languages, including images, media and transcripts.

Quick checklist

  • Does the page start with the task or most important information?
  • Are sentences short and written in active, positive voice?
  • Are headings descriptive, in sentence case, and scannable?
  • Are lists used for steps, options, or key points (≤7 items)?
  • Is link text descriptive and unique? Are downloads labelled?
  • Is content inclusive and accessible (alt text, transcripts, plain language)?
  • Is the French version equivalent and published at the same time?
  • Is the title tag and meta description set and meaningful?
