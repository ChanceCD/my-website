Introduction

The Canada.ca Content Style Guide is the foundation for creating clear, accessible, and user-focused web content for the Government of Canada. Its purpose is to ensure a consistent and trustworthy experience for all users across Canada.ca.

Why it matters:

  • Trust: Consistent style builds public trust in government information.
  • Usability: Clear content helps people find what they need quickly and easily.
  • Accessibility: It ensures that content is usable by people with disabilities.

This training will walk you through the essential elements of the style guide, helping you create content that meets the high standards of Canada.ca.

Core Principles

Write in Plain Language

Communicate clearly and concisely. Use words that your audience will understand. The goal is to make information easy to read, understand, and use. Aim for a grade 6 to 8 reading level.

Avoid

"The department will effectuate the implementation of the new regulations."

Use Instead

"The department will implement the new rules."

Focus on the User

Always write with the user's needs in mind. What are they trying to do? What information do they need? Structure your content to answer their questions directly.

  • Organize content by user task, not by government structure.
  • Use "you" and "your" to speak directly to the user.
  • Start with the most important information.

Design for Accessibility

Content must be accessible to everyone, including people with disabilities who may use assistive technologies. This is a legal and ethical requirement.

  • Write meaningful link text.
  • Use proper heading structures (H1, H2, H3).
  • Provide alternative text for all images.

Writing Style

Active vs. Passive Voice

Use the active voice. It's more direct, concise, and easier to understand. In active voice, the subject performs the action.

Passive

"The application was reviewed by the committee."

Active

"The committee reviewed your application."

Tone of Voice

The tone should be helpful, respectful, and professional. Write as if you're having a conversation with the user.

  • Be positive and encouraging.
  • Use contractions like "you're" or "it's" to sound more natural.
  • Avoid jargon and bureaucratic language.

Formatting

Proper formatting makes content scannable and easier to digest. Users often scan pages for keywords before reading in detail.

Use headings to structure your content logically. Follow a descending order (H1, H2, H3, etc.) and don't skip levels. The page title is always the only H1.

Use bulleted lists for items that don't have a specific order. Use numbered lists for steps in a process or items in a sequence.

Link text should be descriptive and make sense out of context. It should tell the user where they will go if they click the link.

Avoid

"For more information, click here."

Use Instead

"Find out more about applying for a passport."

Accessibility (WCAG)

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are the international standard. Canada.ca must meet level AA.

Alternative Text (Alt Text)

All meaningful images must have descriptive alt text. This text is read aloud by screen readers, providing context for users who cannot see the image.

Example Alt Text: "A family smiles while having a picnic in a sunny park."

Tables

Use tables only for presenting data, not for layout. Ensure tables have proper headers (``) to define rows and columns, which helps screen reader users understand the data's structure.
